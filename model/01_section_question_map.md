# 01 — Section Question Map

Each section has a primary investor question, secondary questions, optional visual criteria, free diagnostic framing, and paid fix triggers.

## Cover

Primary question: Can investors understand the company immediately?

Secondary questions:
- What does the company do?
- Who is it for?
- What category is it in?
- What is the core value proposition?
- Does the framing create enough interest to keep reading?

Free diagnostic framing: The cover should make the company understandable in seconds. If investors have to infer the category, customer, or value proposition, the deck starts with unnecessary friction.

Paid fix triggers: sharper category language, clearer customer targeting, stronger one-line value proposition, reduced jargon, stronger title/subhead pairing.

## Problem

Primary question: Is this a real, painful, specific problem?

Secondary questions:
- What specific problem exists?
- Who specifically has this problem?
- Why is this problem painful or costly?
- Why is this worth solving now?

Free diagnostic framing: The problem should feel specific, urgent, and expensive enough to justify a new company. Generic pain language weakens investor conviction.

Paid fix triggers: sharper customer specificity, clearer pain or cost, stronger status quo comparison, stronger urgency, more concrete examples or proof points.

## Solution

Primary question: Does the solution directly and credibly solve the problem?

Secondary questions:
- What is the solution at a high level?
- How does it directly address the problem?
- Why is this approach meaningfully better?
- What key benefit does the customer get?
- Is the solution easy to visualize?

Product visual criterion: A screenshot, product shot, workflow graphic, or simple mockup can improve clarity if it helps investors visualize the solution quickly. It should clarify the product rather than decorate the slide.

Free diagnostic framing: The solution should be simple enough to understand quickly and specific enough to feel meaningfully better than the current alternative.

Paid fix triggers: clearer one-line description, stronger connection to the problem, sharper differentiation, clearer customer benefit, better visual explanation.

## Product

Primary question: Can investors understand what the product does and why users would want it?

Secondary questions:
- What does the product actually do?
- How does it work in practice?
- What are the most important capabilities?
- What proof suggests the product can deliver?
- Does the slide make the product easy to visualize?
- If a visual is present, does it clarify the product rather than decorate the slide?

Product visual criterion: A clear product visual is a secondary clarity enhancer, not a primary investment proof point. Useful formats include screenshots, product photos, workflow diagrams, architecture diagrams, output examples, and simple mockups.

Free diagnostic framing: The product section should make the product tangible. Investors should be able to see what exists, how it works, and why customers would care.

Paid fix triggers: clearer product explanation, better workflow sequencing, stronger proof that it can deliver, clearer capability hierarchy, more legible or more explanatory product visual.

## Why Now

Primary question: Why is this opportunity newly possible, urgent, or inevitable now?

Secondary questions:
- What has changed to make this possible now?
- What market or technology shift creates urgency?
- Why was this not feasible or obvious earlier?

Free diagnostic framing: The Why Now section should explain why this company can emerge now, not just why the problem has existed for a long time.

Paid fix triggers: clearer timing inflection, stronger technology or market shift, more credible urgency, clearer reason incumbents have not already solved it.

## Market

Primary question: Can this become a venture-scale company?

Secondary questions:
- Who is the target customer?
- How large is the market opportunity?
- Why is this market attractive for a venture-scale business?
- Which wedge or segment matters first?
- How does the company expand from the wedge into a larger opportunity?

Free diagnostic framing: The market section should show both a credible starting point and a path to a much larger outcome. A big TAM number alone is not enough.

Paid fix triggers: clearer beachhead, bottom-up market logic, stronger expansion path, better venture-scale reasoning, more credible customer segmentation.

## Business Model

Primary question: Can this become an attractive, scalable business?

Secondary questions:
- How does the company make money?
- Who pays and for what?
- Why is the pricing model sensible?
- What could drive strong unit economics?
- What could make revenue expand over time?

Free diagnostic framing: The business model should make it clear who pays, why they pay, and why the model can become attractive at scale.

Paid fix triggers: clearer buyer/payer distinction, pricing logic, gross margin or unit economics logic, expansion revenue logic, stronger link between value delivered and price charged.

## GTM

Primary question: Can the company acquire customers repeatably and credibly?

Secondary questions:
- Who is the initial buyer or user?
- How will the company acquire customers?
- Why is this go-to-market approach credible?
- What is the initial wedge into the market?
- What makes distribution easier, cheaper, or more defensible over time?

Free diagnostic framing: The GTM section should make customer acquisition feel plausible, focused, and repeatable rather than aspirational.

Paid fix triggers: clearer initial buyer, sharper channel strategy, more credible wedge, stronger sales motion logic, better explanation of acquisition efficiency.

## Traction

Primary question: Is there real evidence of market pull?

Secondary questions:
- What evidence shows real progress?
- What proof suggests market pull or customer demand?
- What milestones have been achieved?
- What is the strongest concrete signal so far?
- Are the strongest metrics highlighted clearly?
- Does the slide separate activity from durable demand?

Free diagnostic framing: Traction should show evidence that customers, users, partners, or buyers are pulling the company forward. Activity alone is weaker than durable demand.

Paid fix triggers: better metric selection, stronger proof of pull, clearer growth/retention/engagement evidence, stronger milestone framing, better distinction between usage, revenue, pipeline, and customer love.

## Competition

Primary question: Why will this company win against alternatives?

Secondary questions:
- What alternatives do customers use today?
- Why is this company differentiated?
- Why is the current status quo insufficient?
- Why will this not be easily copied?
- What makes the company meaningfully better for the target customer?

Free diagnostic framing: The competition section should show that the company understands the real alternatives and has a credible reason to win.

Paid fix triggers: more accurate alternative set, sharper differentiation, stronger status quo comparison, clearer positioning, more credible anti-copying logic.

## Moat

Primary question: What advantage compounds over time?

Secondary questions:
- What could make the advantage durable?
- What improves as the company grows?
- Why does this become harder to replicate over time?
- Is the moat credible today or only theoretical?
- Does the company get stronger with more customers, data, usage, integrations, workflow depth, or brand?

Free diagnostic framing: The moat section should explain why the company becomes harder to compete with over time. Generic claims about data, AI, or network effects are not enough.

Paid fix triggers: clearer compounding mechanism, better evidence of durability, distinction between feature advantage and moat, more credible data/network/workflow lock-in logic, less generic defensibility language.

## Team

Primary question: Why is this the right team to build this company?

Secondary questions:
- Why is this team well suited to win?
- What unique insight, credibility, or experience does the team have?
- Why are these founders the right people for this problem?
- Does the team reduce the specific execution risks of this company?
- Does the team have relevant domain, product, GTM, recruiting, or technical credibility?

Free diagnostic framing: The team section should prove that the founders are unusually suited to this specific opportunity, not just broadly impressive.

Paid fix triggers: stronger founder-market fit, sharper domain credibility, clearer product-building credibility, stronger GTM or customer-access advantage, better mapping of team strengths to company risks.

## Financials

Primary question: Does the plan make sense?

Secondary questions:
- What assumptions drive the forecast?
- Are revenue, cost, and growth assumptions credible?
- Does the plan match the GTM motion?
- Does the capital plan support the next milestone?
- Are the numbers internally consistent?

Free diagnostic framing: Financials should make the growth plan feel credible and internally consistent, not overly precise or detached from the GTM reality.

Paid fix triggers: more credible assumptions, clearer revenue drivers, better cost logic, stronger milestone linkage, simplified presentation.

## Ask

Primary question: Is the financing tied to credible value-creating milestones?

Secondary questions:
- How much is being raised?
- What milestones will this funding achieve?
- What does success look like after this round?
- Why is this the right amount to raise now?
- Does the ask set up the next financing or business milestone?

Free diagnostic framing: The ask should make the raise feel deliberate. Investors should understand what the money unlocks and why those milestones matter.

Paid fix triggers: clearer raise amount, better use of funds, milestone linkage, stronger next-round logic, sharper outcome definition.
