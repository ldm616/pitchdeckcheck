# 05 — BVP Precedent Examples

Use these examples selectively. Do not dump them into every report.

## LinkedIn — Team, Network, Trust, Category Formation

Investor signal: Founder credibility, prior company-building experience, and network relevance mattered because the opportunity depended on trust, adoption, and network formation.

Generalized rule: Investors may overweight founder quality when product behavior, monetization, or category formation is still early.

Deck implication: A Team slide should explain why the team has a specific advantage in building trust, attracting users, and forming the market.

Useful for: Team, Market, Why Now, Moat, GTM.

## Shopify — Product Pull, Simplicity, Capital Efficiency

Investor signal: Shopify showed signs of customer pull, organic growth, MRR growth, GMV growth, product simplicity, and strong execution before heavy institutional scaling.

Generalized rule: Investors respond strongly when customer pull appears before major paid acquisition or heavy fundraising.

Deck implication: A Traction slide should separate organic pull from paid growth. A Product slide should show why the product is simple, useful, and adopted by real customers.

Useful for: Product, Traction, Business Model, GTM, Team.

## PagerDuty — Retention, Expansion, Category Leadership

Investor signal: PagerDuty’s investor appeal came from strong growth, low churn, expansion, and the possibility of becoming a broader operational platform.

Generalized rule: Investors respond strongly when a company shows that customers not only adopt the product but expand usage over time.

Deck implication: Traction should highlight retention, expansion, usage depth, and signs that the product can become a system of record or core workflow layer.

Useful for: Traction, Product, Business Model, Moat, Market.

## Mindbody — Market Expansion Risk

Investor signal: The big investor question was whether the company could expand beyond its initial niche into broader salon, spa, wellness, and related markets.

Generalized rule: Investors often care less about whether the starting segment is large and more about whether the path from wedge to large market is credible.

Deck implication: A Market slide should show beachhead, adjacent expansion, and why the company can move from narrow wedge to venture-scale opportunity.

Useful for: Market, GTM, Business Model, Traction.

## Wix — Vision Despite Business Model Risk

Investor signal: Wix had meaningful business model uncertainty, but the product vision, market shift, and team/product strength made the opportunity compelling.

Generalized rule: Investors may tolerate one major unresolved risk if the upside, timing, product insight, and team strength are unusually compelling.

Deck implication: If a deck has a known weakness, make the compensating strength obvious. Do not hide the risk; overpower it with credible upside and execution evidence.

Useful for: Why Now, Product, Team, Business Model, Risk.

## ServiceTitan — Domain-Specific Founder-Market Fit

Investor signal: ServiceTitan benefited from founders with direct relevance to the contractor/home-services market and a clear understanding of vertical workflow pain.

Generalized rule: In domain-specific or vertical markets, founder-market fit can be as important as early product maturity.

Deck implication: A Team slide should show why the founders understand the buyer, workflow, and market better than generic software founders.

Useful for: Team, Problem, Product, GTM, Market.

## Toast — Vertical Workflow and System-of-Record Potential

Investor signal: Toast’s appeal came from solving a specific vertical workflow with potential to become a central operating platform.

Generalized rule: Vertical SaaS companies become more compelling when the product can expand from point solution into operating system.

Deck implication: Product and Market slides should show how the company starts with urgent workflow pain and expands into a broader platform opportunity.

Useful for: Product, Market, Business Model, Moat, GTM.

## Twilio — Developer Adoption and Platform Potential

Investor signal: Twilio’s strength came from developer adoption, API-first simplicity, and platform expansion.

Generalized rule: Developer-tool companies need to prove ease of adoption, workflow integration, and expansion from initial use case to broader platform.

Deck implication: A Product slide should show how developers adopt the product quickly. A Moat slide should show how usage, integrations, and platform breadth compound.

Useful for: Product, GTM, Traction, Moat, Market.
