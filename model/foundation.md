## Purpose

Foundation defines the durable investment principles that explain what makes a startup compelling to venture investors.

It is the root artifact for Pitch Deck Check.

Foundation is not a pitch deck framework.

Foundation is not a scoring rubric.

Foundation is not report wording.

Foundation defines the underlying investment logic that all other product artifacts should reflect.

---

## Core Idea

A pitch deck is evidence for an investment thesis.

Pitch Deck Check evaluates whether the deck supports a compelling investment thesis given the company's current stage.

The deck is not evaluated as a collection of slides.

The deck is evaluated as an argument that the company could become an exceptional investment.

---

## Venture Investment Objective

Venture investors seek opportunities to invest in companies that can create extraordinary long-term value.

An exceptional venture investment is usually a company that can become the dominant provider of meaningful customer value in a sufficiently large market.

The largest outcomes come from companies that:

- identify a large and durable customer value gap
- create a compelling value proposition
- build a product that realizes that value
- get customers to adopt the product despite inertia
- scale distribution efficiently
- defend their position over time
- capture a meaningful portion of the value they create

---

## Value Gaps

Great startup opportunities begin with value gaps.

A value gap exists when customers receive substantially less value from current alternatives than they want or need.

Current alternatives may include:

- incumbents
- manual work
- internal tools
- homegrown solutions
- consultants or service providers
- outdated workflows
- doing nothing

A value gap may be economic, experiential or both.

Examples of value gaps include solutions that are:

- too expensive
- too slow
- too unreliable
- too difficult to use
- too error-prone
- too risky
- too inconvenient
- too limited
- too impersonal
- too inaccessible

Problems matter because value gaps have consequences.

The greater the consequence of the value gap, the stronger the motivation for customers to change.

---

## Customer Change

Customers are reluctant to change.

This is true for consumers, SMBs, enterprises and government agencies.

Customers usually change only when the perceived value gained from switching exceeds the cost, risk and effort of changing behavior.

A marginally better solution is rarely enough.

A compelling startup must show why customers will prefer the new approach strongly enough to overcome inertia.

---

## Solution and Product

Solution and Product are distinct.

Solution defines the ideal future state for the customer.

Product is the company's implementation of that future state.

A strong Solution shows that the founders understand what customers actually want.

A strong Product shows that the company can make that desired future real.

Not all solution insights are equal.

A better founder insight often identifies a more complete version of the desired future state.

For example, the strongest solution may include workflow, distribution, integrations, services, ecosystem or business model components that narrower competitors miss.

The more complete the solution insight, the more compelling the investment thesis can become.

---

## Promise and Proof

Fundability depends on the appropriate combination of promise and proof for the company's stage.

Promise is the credibility of what the company could become.

Proof is evidence that the promise is becoming true.

Early-stage investments are usually driven by exceptional promise because objective proof is limited.

Later-stage investments are driven increasingly by proof.

Successful startups progressively convert promise into proof.

Proof does not merely add to promise.

Proof replaces the need to rely on promise.

For example, early investors may need to believe that consumers will adopt a new behavior.

Later investors should see empirical evidence that consumers already are adopting that behavior.

---

## Stage-Appropriate Evidence

The investment thesis should remain coherent across stages.

What changes is the amount and quality of evidence expected.

At Idea / Pre-Product stage, investors primarily evaluate the credibility of the insight, the team, the value gap, the proposed future state and the size of the opportunity.

At Product / Pre-Revenue stage, investors expect early proof that the company can build the product and that customers recognize the value.

At Early Revenue stage, investors expect proof that customers will pay and that adoption can become repeatable.

At Growth stage, investors expect proof that the company can scale efficiently, defend its position and capture value over time.

The questions investors ask remain broadly consistent.

The expected evidence changes.

---

## Fundability

Fundability is the degree to which a company appears compelling to investors given its current combination of promise and proof.

Fundability is not purely subjective.

Different investors may weigh opportunities differently, but exceptional companies tend to share common underlying traits.

A company becomes more fundable when its deck convincingly reduces uncertainty around the most important parts of the investment thesis.

A company becomes less fundable when important claims remain unsupported, unclear or inconsistent with its stage.

Pitch Deck Check should evaluate fundability relative to stage, not against a single universal standard.

---

## Dominance and Defense

Great venture outcomes usually require more than creating value.

The company must be able to defend its ability to create, deliver and capture that value over time.

Defensibility may come from many sources, including:

- technology
- data
- workflow integration
- network effects
- switching costs
- distribution
- brand
- ecosystem
- operational complexity
- regulatory position
- customer trust

Moats may evolve over time.

An early moat may be a product insight, technical advantage or go-to-market wedge.

A later moat may become brand, scale, network effects, data advantage or ecosystem control.

A compelling investment thesis should explain why the company can remain advantaged as competitors respond.

---

## Value Capture

Creating customer value is not enough.

The company must also capture a meaningful portion of that value through a scalable business model.

A strong business model connects customer value creation to company revenue, margin and enterprise value.

The best companies create more value for customers than they capture, while still capturing enough value to build a large and durable business.

---

## Role of the Pitch Deck

A pitch deck is an efficient argument for why a company could become an exceptional investment.

The pitch deck does not need to prove everything.

It must provide enough promise and proof for the company's stage to justify the next investor conversation.

The deck should help investors understand:

- what value gap exists
- why customers care
- why now is the right time
- what future state the company envisions
- how the product realizes that future state
- why the team can execute
- why customers will adopt
- why the company can win
- why the market is large enough
- why the business can capture value
- why the current financing can increase the company's value

## Product Implication

Pitch Deck Check should not merely critique slides.

It should evaluate how convincingly the deck supports an investment thesis.

Every downstream artifact should preserve this distinction.

The Investor Framework defines the questions that test the thesis.

The Company Context artifact defines the stage-appropriate evidence expected.

The Scoring Rubric defines how well the deck supports the thesis.

The Report Specification defines how to communicate the assessment to founders.

The Improvement Framework defines how to help founders strengthen the thesis.

---

## Stability Principle

Foundation should change rarely.

Questions, rubrics, prompts, reports and implementation may evolve.

Foundation should change only when the underlying investment principles need to be revised.
