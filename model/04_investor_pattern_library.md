# 04 — Investor Pattern Library

This file captures generalized investor standards extracted from memos, investor commentary, and founder/deck evaluation patterns.

## Founder / Team Patterns

### Founder-market fit

Investors respond strongly when founders have a specific, credible reason to understand the market better than outsiders.

Strong signals:
- direct experience with the customer
- direct experience with the workflow
- prior work in the industry
- personal exposure to the problem
- deep network in the market
- repeated customer discovery before building
- insight that feels non-obvious but grounded

Weak signals:
- generic impressive logos
- vague “experienced team” claims
- advisors listed without clear involvement
- credentials unrelated to the company’s main risks

### Execution velocity

Investors like evidence that the team learns and ships quickly.

Strong signals:
- rapid iteration
- early launch despite constraints
- customer feedback loops
- short cycles from insight to product
- measurable progress with little capital

### Commercial instinct

Investors value founders who understand how the company will sell, price, distribute, and expand.

Strong signals:
- clear buyer understanding
- early revenue or LOIs
- pricing logic
- distribution insight
- repeatable sales motion hypothesis
- ability to explain buying urgency

## Problem Patterns

Specificity beats abstraction. Strong problem slides name the specific customer, painful workflow, cost/consequence, urgency, and current workaround.

Pain must be costly enough to create budget, urgency, or behavior change.

## Solution Patterns

A strong solution should feel like a direct response to the problem, not a general product pitch.

“Better” should be more than faster, cheaper, or AI-powered. It should produce a materially better customer outcome.

## Product Patterns

Investors need to visualize the product. Strong product slides use screenshots, product shots, workflows, output examples, architecture diagrams, or annotated journeys.

Product capabilities should map to outcomes: capability → customer action → business outcome.

## Market Patterns

Big TAM is not enough. Investors want to know where the company starts, why that segment is urgent, how it expands, and why the expanded opportunity is venture-scale.

Strong market logic: beachhead → adjacent expansion → category-scale opportunity.

## Traction Patterns

Pull beats activity. Strong traction shows that customers are pulling the company forward.

Strong signals include revenue growth, retention, repeat usage, expansion, inbound demand, customer referrals, usage intensity, pipeline quality, willingness to pay, low churn, and fast sales cycles for the category.

## Business Model Patterns

Value capture must match value creation. Investors want pricing that makes sense relative to customer value created.

## GTM Patterns

Investors want a focused starting wedge: specific buyer, specific channel, specific beachhead, credible early access, repeatable sales hypothesis.

## Competition Patterns

Competition includes the status quo: spreadsheets, agencies, manual work, internal tools, consultants, incumbent software, and doing nothing.

Differentiation must matter to the customer.

## Moat Patterns

Moats compound. Possible compounding mechanisms include data advantage, network effects, workflow lock-in, integrations, brand trust, proprietary distribution, ecosystem, operational scale, regulatory approvals, and switching costs.

Data is not automatically a moat. It only matters if it is proprietary, hard to collect, improves the product, creates better outcomes, and becomes harder for others to replicate.

## Ask / Financing Patterns

The raise should map to milestones: amount raised, use of funds, milestones, proof needed for next round, runway logic.
